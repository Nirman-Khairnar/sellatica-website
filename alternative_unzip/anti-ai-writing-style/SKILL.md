# Anti-AI Writing Style — Complete Field Guide

This skill teaches you to write (and edit) text that does not read as machine-generated. It is built from the most comprehensive public taxonomy of AI writing tells in existence — the Wikipedia "Signs of AI Writing" guide, maintained by researchers and editors who have read thousands of examples.

The core problem with AI writing: LLMs regress to the mean. They omit specific, unusual, nuanced facts (statistically rare) and replace them with generic, positive descriptions (statistically common). The highly specific "inventor of the first train-coupling device" becomes "a revolutionary titan of industry." The portrait fades from a sharp photograph into a blurry, generic sketch — simultaneously less specific and more exaggerated.

Fix this by going the other direction: more specific, less grand.

---

## PART 1 — BANNED VOCABULARY

These words appear so disproportionately in AI-generated text that a single usage raises suspicion. Multiple in one paragraph is nearly conclusive.

### Core AI vocabulary (use never or almost never)
- **additionally** (especially to begin a sentence)
- **align with / resonate with**
- **crucial / vital / pivotal / key** (as adjectives for importance)
- **delve**
- **emphasizing / underscoring / highlighting** (as participle phrases)
- **enduring / lasting / ongoing** (when describing legacy)
- **enhance / enhancing**
- **evolving landscape** (as abstract noun)
- **fostering**
- **garner**
- **groundbreaking** (in figurative sense)
- **highlight** (as verb meaning "to show the significance of")
- **interplay**
- **intricate / intricacies**
- **landscape** (as abstract noun — "the business landscape")
- **pivotal**
- **renowned**
- **rich** (figurative — "rich cultural heritage")
- **robust**
- **seamless**
- **showcase / showcasing**
- **tapestry** (as abstract noun — "a tapestry of experiences")
- **testament** ("a testament to...")
- **underscore** (as verb — "this underscores the importance of")
- **valuable** (as vague praise — "valuable insights")
- **vibrant**

### Promotional / puffery words
- boasts a
- breathtaking
- commitment to
- exemplifies
- in the heart of
- natural beauty
- nestled
- profound (figurative)
- stands as / serves as (in place of "is")

### Weasel / vague attribution words
- experts argue / observers have noted / industry reports suggest
- some critics argue
- several sources / publications (when you only have one or two)
- such as (before exhaustive lists framed as examples)

## PART 2 — CONTENT PATTERNS TO AVOID

### 2.1 Inflating Significance and Legacy

**The problem:** Attaching statements about how arbitrary things represent or contribute to something grander. LLMs do this constantly, even for mundane topics.

**Banned constructions:**
- "marking a pivotal moment in the evolution of..."
- "setting the stage for..."
- "represents a shift toward..."
- "part of a broader movement to..."
- "contributing to the socio-economic development of..."
- "reflects the enduring legacy of..."
- "symbolizing its ongoing relevance..."

**Before:**
> The factory was built in 1961, marking a pivotal moment in the region's industrial development and setting the stage for decades of economic growth.

**After:**
> The factory was built in 1961 and employed around 400 people by 1970.

**Rule:** If you find yourself writing about what something "represents" or "marks," stop and ask: what does it actually do? Write that instead.

---

### 2.2 Asserting Notability Instead of Demonstrating It

**The problem:** Instead of letting specific coverage speak for itself, AI lists sources as proof of importance — sometimes without explaining what those sources actually said.

**Banned constructions:**
- "Her work has been featured in The New York Times, BBC, Wired, and Forbes."
- "He maintains an active social media presence."
- "The project has received significant independent coverage."

**Before:**
> Her insights have been featured in Wired, Refinery29, and other prominent media outlets, establishing her as a thought leader in the space.

**After:**
> In a 2024 Wired profile, she argued that social media's attention economy actively rewards bad reasoning.

**Rule:** Name the source AND what it said. If you can't, cut it.

---

### 2.3 Superficial Analysis with -ing Phrases

**The problem:** Tacking present participle phrases onto sentences to manufacture depth that isn't there. The sentence states a fact, then explains "what it means" in the most generic terms possible.

**Pattern:** [Fact], [present participle] [vague significance].

**Banned phrases:**
- "...highlighting the collaborative nature of..."
- "...emphasizing the importance of..."
- "...reflecting broader trends in..."
- "...contributing to a richer understanding of..."
- "...fostering a sense of..."
- "...cultivating a culture of..."
- "...underscoring the community's commitment to..."

**Before:**
> The bridge was completed in 2004, reflecting the city's ongoing commitment to infrastructure and highlighting the collaborative spirit of its residents.

**After:**
> The bridge was completed in 2004. Construction took three years and cost $40 million over budget.

**Rule:** After a fact, ask: "So what — specifically?" Give a concrete answer or cut the phrase entirely.

---

### 2.4 Promotional and Advertisement-like Language

**The problem:** LLMs default to tourist-brochure or press-release mode, especially when writing about places, companies, or cultural subjects.

**Before:**
> Nestled within the breathtaking coastal region, the company stands as a vibrant hub of innovation, offering customers a rich and seamless experience unlike any other.

**After:**
> The company makes inventory software for mid-size retailers.

**Rule:** Read your text aloud. If it sounds like it could be a TV commercial or a tourism flyer, rewrite it as if you were explaining it to a friend.

---

### 2.5 Vague Attributions (Weasel Wording)

**The problem:** Attributing claims or opinions to unnamed, vague authorities.

**Before:**
> Experts have noted that remote work has transformed modern business. Industry observers suggest this trend will continue.

**After:**
> A 2023 McKinsey survey of 13,000 workers found that 87% prefer some form of flexible work arrangement.

**Rule:** Every claim needs a real, named source — or it gets cut or reframed as your own observation.

---

### 2.6 Formulaic "Challenges and Future Prospects" Endings

**The problem:** A rigid formula: acknowledge the subject's strengths, then pivot with "Despite its [positive trait], [subject] faces challenges..." followed by vague optimism about potential initiatives.

**Banned constructions:**
- "Despite its success, X faces several challenges including..."
- "Despite these challenges, X continues to thrive..."
- "Future developments in [technology] could enhance X's relevance..."
- Any section literally titled "Challenges and Future Prospects" or "Future Outlook"

**Before:**
> Despite its industrial prosperity, the city faces challenges typical of urban areas, including congestion and aging infrastructure. With ongoing initiatives, it continues to thrive as an integral part of the region.

**After:**
> The city's rail network handles 40% more passengers than it was designed for. The mayor's office has proposed a $1.2 billion expansion, which is currently stalled in the state legislature.

**Rule:** Specific problems. Specific solutions. Real status. No formulaic hope.

## PART 3 — LANGUAGE AND GRAMMAR TELLS

### 3.1 Copula Avoidance (Replacing "is/are" with elaborate alternatives)

**The problem:** LLM edits replace simple "is/are/has" constructions with fancier verbs to sound more sophisticated. Studies show measurable drops in "is" and "are" frequency in AI-edited academic writing.

**Banned substitutions:**
- "serves as" (instead of "is")
- "stands as" (instead of "is")
- "marks" (instead of "is")
- "represents" (instead of "is")
- "boasts" (instead of "has")
- "features" (instead of "has")
- "offers" (instead of "has")

**Before:**
> Gallery 825 serves as LAAA's exhibition space for contemporary art. The gallery features four separate spaces and offers a rotating program of emerging artists.

**After:**
> Gallery 825 is LAAA's exhibition space for contemporary art. It has four rooms and shows emerging artists on a rotating basis.

**Rule:** Default to "is," "are," and "has." Only use alternatives when they add specific meaning.

---

### 3.2 Negative Parallelisms

**The problem:** LLMs construct "not only... but also..." and "it's not just X, it's Y" patterns to appear balanced and thoughtful. These read as performative depth.

**Banned constructions:**
- "Not only X, but Y"
- "It's not just about X; it's about Y"
- "No X, no Y — just Z"
- "Not a [noun] but a [grander noun]"

**Before:**
> This isn't just a product launch; it's a statement about the future of work. Not a tool, not a platform — a paradigm shift.

**After:**
> The company released a new project management app. It integrates with Slack and costs $12 per user per month.

---

### 3.3 Rule of Three

**The problem:** LLMs dramatically overuse three-item lists — three adjectives, three noun phrases, three short clauses — to appear comprehensive. One instance is fine. A pattern is a tell.

**Before:**
> The event features keynote sessions, panel discussions, and networking opportunities. Attendees will learn, connect, and grow. Speakers are dynamic, insightful, and inspiring.

**After:**
> The event has two keynote sessions and a panel on AI regulation. Speakers include the head of policy at Anthropic and two researchers from Stanford.

**Rule:** When you write a list of three, ask: do all three add information, or are any just padding to hit the triad? Cut the padding.

---

### 3.4 False Ranges ("from X to Y")

**The problem:** Using "from X to Y" constructions where no actual scale exists between the endpoints. LLMs do this to sound sweeping and comprehensive.

**"From X to Y" is valid when:** there's a real scale with X and Y at opposite ends — from 1990 to 2000, from mild to severe, from seed to tree.

**"From X to Y" is a false range when:** the endpoints are just loosely related things with no coherent scale between them.

**Before:**
> Our platform handles everything from onboarding to insights, from automation to analytics, from startups to enterprise.

**After:**
> Our platform automates onboarding workflows and tracks key metrics. It scales from solo founders to companies with 5,000+ employees.

---

### 3.5 Elegant Variation (Synonym Cycling)

**The problem:** LLMs use repetition penalties, so they cycle through synonyms for the same person or concept instead of repeating the name. This reads as oddly formal and literary in contexts where it doesn't belong.

**Before:**
> Sarah launched the initiative in 2019. The founder saw an opportunity the market had missed. The entrepreneur's bold vision attracted early investors. The visionary leader later expanded to three new markets.

**After:**
> Sarah launched the initiative in 2019. She saw an opportunity in underserved rural markets and raised $2M in seed funding by 2021.

## PART 4 — FORMATTING AND STYLE TELLS

### 4.1 Overuse of Boldface

AI output bolds nearly everything, especially in a "key takeaways" pattern where every instance of a chosen term gets bolded.

**Rule:** Bold only what is genuinely critical and would cause misunderstanding if missed. One or two bolds per section maximum. If you're bolding more than that, you're writing an AI list.

---

### 4.2 Bullet Lists with Bold Headers

**The problem:** AI output almost always structures information as:
```
- **Header:** Description text that explains the header.
- **Another Header:** Another description.
```

This is one of the most reliable AI formatting signatures.

**Before:**
> - **Speed:** Code generation is significantly faster, reducing friction.
> - **Quality:** Output quality has been enhanced through improved training.
> - **Adoption:** Usage continues to grow, reflecting broader trends.

**After (prose):**
> AI coding tools can speed up boilerplate generation. Output quality has improved since 2022. Adoption is rising, though rates vary significantly by industry.

**Rule:** Prefer prose for explanatory content. Use bullets only for genuinely list-like items (steps, requirements, options) — not for wrapping mini-essays.

---

### 4.3 Emoji in Headings and Bullets

Using emoji to decorate section headers or bullet points is a strong AI signal, especially in professional or analytical writing.

**Rule:** No emoji in headings, bullets, or as decoration. If your audience explicitly expects emoji (social copy, casual channels), they're acceptable inline — not as structural markers.

---

### 4.4 Overuse of Em Dashes

LLMs overuse em dashes to create a punchy, sales-like rhythm — often mimicking persuasive copywriting in contexts where it doesn't fit.

**Before:**
> The results — while preliminary — suggest a clear trend. The data — drawn from three independent studies — confirms what practitioners have long suspected — that the old model is broken.

**After:**
> Preliminary results suggest a clear trend. Data from three independent studies confirm what practitioners have suspected: the old model has structural problems.

**Rule:** Use em dashes sparingly. If you've used more than two in a paragraph, rewrite. Commas, colons, and periods usually serve better.

---

### 4.5 Title Case Section Headers

AI almost always capitalizes every main word in section headings (Title Case). This reads as corporate or auto-generated.

**Before:**
> ## Strategic Negotiations and Global Partnerships
> ## High-Stakes Deals: Glencore, China, and Russia

**After:**
> ## Strategic negotiations
> ## High-stakes deals

**Rule:** Use sentence case for headings unless there's a brand or style-guide reason not to.

---

### 4.6 Unnecessary Tables

AI creates small tables for data that reads fine as a sentence or two.

**Rule:** Only use a table when you have three or more attributes across three or more items and the comparison is the point. Otherwise, write a sentence.

---

## PART 5 — COLLABORATIVE COMMUNICATION TELLS

These are phrases AI uses to address the person it's talking to. They often leak into pasted AI content.

### Never use:
- "I hope this helps"
- "Of course!"
- "Certainly!"
- "You're absolutely right!"
- "Great question!"
- "Would you like me to..."
- "Is there anything else I can help with?"
- "Let me know if..."
- "Here is a [thing]" (as an opener)
- "Feel free to reach out"

These phrases are not just annoying — they are forensic evidence of AI output.

---

## PART 6 — KNOWLEDGE HEDGING TELLS

AI inserts disclaimers when it doesn't know something. These are easy to spot:

### Never use:
- "While specific details are limited..."
- "Based on available information..."
- "While information is not widely documented..."
- "As of my last knowledge update..."
- "Specific details may vary..."
- "[Person] maintains a low profile and keeps personal details private." (AI says this when it doesn't have the info)

**Rule:** If you don't know something, either find out or say clearly that it's unknown and why. Don't pad with hedging language.

---

## PART 7 — HISTORICAL / OLDER AI TELLS

These were common in 2022–2024 AI output. Less frequent now, but still appear:

- **Didactic disclaimers:** "It's important to note that..." / "It's crucial to remember that..."
- **Conclusion sections:** Starting a paragraph with "In summary..." or "In conclusion..." or "Overall..."
- **Prompt refusals:** "As an AI language model, I cannot..." (Now rare)
- **Abrupt cutoffs:** Text that just stops mid-thought (sign of token limit being hit)

---

## PART 8 — WHAT IS NOT A SIGN OF AI WRITING

Avoid over-flagging these as AI tells — they're not reliable indicators:

- **Perfect grammar** — many humans write well
- **Academic or "fancy" vocabulary** — unusual words are actually less likely in AI output
- **Formal register mixed with casual** — this is normal for technical writers and neurodivergent people
- **"Bland" or "clinical" prose** — AI defaults to *effusive* prose, not flat prose
- **Conjunctions at sentence starts** — this is normal and stylistically acceptable

## PART 9 — SELF-CHECK PROCESS

After writing or editing any content, run this internal review:

### Vocabulary check
Search your text for every word in Part 1. Replace or cut each one.

### Significance check
Find every sentence that claims something is important, pivotal, transformative, or significant. Ask: "What specifically happened?" Replace with the specific fact.

### Attribution check
Find every claim attributed to "experts," "observers," "industry reports," or "some sources." Replace with a named source and what it actually said. If you can't, cut the claim.

### Formatting check
- More than 2 bolds in a paragraph? Reduce.
- Bullet list with bold headers? Rewrite as prose.
- Em dashes everywhere? Replace with commas, colons, or periods.
- Section headers in Title Case? Switch to Sentence case.

### Voice check
Read it aloud. Does it sound like a person? If it sounds like a polished brochure, it's too AI. Vary sentence length. Include at least one specific, concrete detail per paragraph.

### Final anti-AI pass
Ask yourself: "What makes this obviously AI-generated?" Answer honestly. Fix those things. Then ask: "Does a real human voice come through?" If not, add specificity, vary the rhythm, cut the grand claims.

## PART 10 — QUICK REFERENCE

### Patterns that almost always signal AI:
1. Multiple "AI vocabulary" words in one paragraph
2. Bullets with **Bold Header:** Description format
3. "Not only X, but Y" constructions
4. "-ing phrase" at the end of a sentence explaining significance
5. "Despite its [strength], X faces challenges... continues to thrive"
6. Vague attributions to unnamed experts
7. "serves as" / "stands as" instead of "is"
8. Three-part lists used to pad everything

### Replacements at a glance:
| AI phrase | Replace with |
|---|---|
| serves as / stands as | is |
| boasts / features | has |
| marking a pivotal moment | (delete) |
| highlighting the importance of | (delete) |
| experts argue | [Name], [title], said / wrote |
| vibrant / rich / nestled | (delete or replace with specifics) |
| the evolving landscape of | [the industry / the market / the field] |
| a testament to | (delete) |
| Additionally, | (delete or use "Also") |
| In conclusion | (delete) |

This skill is built from [Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing), maintained by WikiProject AI Cleanup — a peer-reviewed field guide based on analysis of thousands of examples of LLM-generated text, supported by academic research including studies published in *Science Advances*, *PNAS*, and proceedings of the Association for Computational Linguistics.

Core insight from the source: "LLMs use statistical algorithms to guess what should come next based on a large corpus of training material. It thus tends to regress to the mean — the result tends toward the most statistically likely result that applies to the widest variety of cases. Specific, unusual, nuanced facts get smoothed over into generic positive descriptions."

The fix: go the other way. Be specific. Be concrete. Be sparse with importance.

## PART 11 — NEGATIVE PARALLELISM (THE #1 AI TELL)

Source: Washington Post analysis of 328,744 ChatGPT messages.
"Not just X, but Y" variations appeared in ~6% of all messages.
That is the single most statistically overused rhetorical pattern in AI writing.

THE CORE PATTERN AND ALL ITS DISGUISES:

Banned — every variation of "It's not X, it's Y":
- "It's not a tool problem. It's a process problem."
- "The issue isn't AI. The issue is the layer underneath."
- "This isn't about technology. It's about systems."
- "Not a hiring problem. An infrastructure problem."
- "It's not just automation. It's transformation."

Banned — the causal variant:
- "Not because the tool is wrong. Because the process is."
- "Not because they didn't try. Because nobody mapped it first."
- "Not because of poor execution. Because of missing structure."

Banned — the question reframe:
- "The question isn't which tool. The question is what process."
- "The challenge isn't adoption. The challenge is the layer underneath."

Banned — cross-sentence reframe (same noun negated then repositioned):
- "Your AI strategy is not working. What is missing is the logic beneath it."
- "The team is not the problem. The system is."

WHY SIMPLY BANNING THE PHRASE DOES NOT WORK:

Blocking "It's not X, it's Y" pushes the model to find a synonymous structure.
"While X is true, Y is paramount" carries the same rhetorical function under
a different surface form.

Effective prohibition targets the BEHAVIOR, not the phrase:
- Prohibit thesis-antithesis patterns
- Prohibit dialectical hedging
- Prohibit rhetorical equivocation

THE REPLACEMENT RULE — AFFIRMATIVE DEFINITION:

Instead of telling the reader what something is NOT,
state what it IS directly and let the contrast be implicit.

❌ "This isn't a tool problem. It's a process problem."
✅ "The process breaks before the tool gets involved."

❌ "The question isn't which AI to buy. It's what the process looks like first."
✅ "Map the process. Then pick the tool."

❌ "Not a people problem. An infrastructure problem."
✅ "The team works fine. The infrastructure they're working through doesn't."

The difference: affirmative definition gives the reader a picture.
Negative parallelism gives them a pivot. Pivots feel like insight.
Pictures create it.

## PART 12 — UNEARNED PROFUNDITY TRANSITIONS

These are dramatic narrative-shift phrases that AI uses to create the feeling
that something important just happened. Nothing important has happened.

Banned phrases:
- "Something shifted."
- "Everything changed."
- "But here's the thing."
- "And then it clicked."
- "That's when I realised."
- "This is the part nobody talks about."
- "Here's what most people miss."
- "The thing is..."
- "At its core..."
- "When you zoom out..."
- "The real question is..."
- "The truth is..."

Why they fail: they announce profundity instead of delivering it.
A human writer who has genuinely realised something just states it.
They do not narrate the moment of realisation.

❌ "That's when I realised — the tool was never the problem."
✅ "The tool was never the problem."

❌ "Here's what most people miss: AI fails before you deploy it."
✅ "AI fails before you deploy it."

Rule: Delete the announcement. Keep the insight. Every time.

## PART 13 — DIPLOMATIC SYMMETRY AND FALSE BALANCE

AI aims for diplomatic balance every time. Human writing is uneven, opinionated,
and asymmetric. The difference is commitment.

Banned patterns:
- "Whether you are a beginner or an expert..."
- "While X is true, Y is also important."
- "On one hand... on the other hand..."
- "Both X and Y have their place."
- "There are benefits and drawbacks to consider."
- "It depends on your specific situation."

These patterns avoid commitment. Every business owner reading LinkedIn can
tell the difference between someone with a real opinion and someone hedging.

The replacement: Take a side. State it. Let the reader disagree.

❌ "Both manual processes and AI have their advantages depending on your needs."
✅ "Manual processes are not a strategy. They are a stopgap."

❌ "While some businesses benefit from AI, others may find traditional methods work."
✅ "The businesses not using AI right now are not being careful. They are falling behind."

## PART 14 — SNAPPY TRIADS (RULE OF THREE ABUSE)

AI uses three-item lists to appear comprehensive. One triad is fine.
A pattern of triads in one post is forensic AI evidence.

Banned patterns:
- Three adjectives in a row: "clear, structured, and actionable"
- Three action verbs in a row: "map, build, and deploy"
- Three-beat motivational cadences: "Think bigger. Act bolder. Move faster."
- Three short parallel sentences used as a kicker: "Know the break. Fix the path. Stop the leak."

The test: if you have written a three-item list anywhere in the last 5 lines,
the next time you instinctively reach for three items, use two or four instead.
Break the rhythm. Humans are not consistent. AI is.

## PART 15 — OVER-NEAT STRUCTURE AND FRICTIONLESS TRANSITIONS

AI text reads like it was revised compulsively but never improvised.
Human writing has tonal shifts, asymmetric pacing, and occasional digressions.

Signs of over-neat structure in LinkedIn posts:
- Every paragraph is the same length
- Every sentence lands a complete thought with equal weight
- Transitions are smooth every single time (no abrupt jumps)
- The post builds to a clean conclusion that ties everything together
- Nothing is left hanging or unresolved

The replacement: deliberate asymmetry.

One short paragraph followed immediately by one long one.
A thought that does not fully resolve before moving to the next.
A sentence that trails into a question without answering it.
An ending that does not tie everything up.

Real writers leave things slightly unfinished.
That incompleteness is what makes readers fill in the gap — and comment.

## PART 16 — THE "SOMETHING + SHIFT" CONSTRUCTION

AI loves to write that something "changed everything" or "shifted the game"
or that there was a turning point that altered a trajectory.

Banned constructions:
- "This changed everything."
- "That one shift made all the difference."
- "Everything was different after that."
- "This is the pivot point most businesses miss."
- "That decision transformed how we work."

These are vague causal claims with no specificity. They create the emotional
shape of a story without the content of one.

Replacement rule: name the specific thing that changed and what the
before and after looked like in concrete terms.

❌ "That one change transformed their operation."
✅ "They stopped routing every delivery confirmation through the owner's phone.
   Calls dropped from 40 a day to 11."

Specific. Measurable. No dramatic framing required.
